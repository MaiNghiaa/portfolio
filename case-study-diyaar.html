01 Background

A super-app for a market we'd never visited

A Somali entrepreneur approached CDSL with a clear vision: one app to unify everyday services — food delivery, grocery ordering, pharmacy, and ride delivery — for users in Somalia. No incumbent super-app. No existing platform to migrate from. Just a contract, a deadline, and a team of 12 people split between Hanoi and Mogadishu.

My role was PM and Software Engineer. I owned the architecture decisions, sprint cadence, client communication (in English), and CI/CD setup. The original estimate was 5 months to full delivery and App Store/Google Play launch.

02 The Problem

The users had daily services. They just couldn't access them reliably.

The Somali market had individual service providers — restaurants, pharmacies, grocery stores — but no unified digital layer. Customers had no consistent way to discover, order, or pay for services on a single platform. The client wanted to own that layer.

From a PM perspective, the challenge wasn't just building the app. It was deciding what to build first, in what order, for a market we couldn't directly test with — while keeping a 12-person cross-cultural team aligned and a client 4 time zones away satisfied.

03 Constraints

Fixed deadline. Scope uncertainty. 4-hour time gap.

The real constraints

Hard deadline — App Store + Google Play launch was tied to a client marketing campaign. Slipping wasn't an option.  ·  Cross-timezone team — Vietnam (UTC+7) and Somalia (UTC+3) meant a 4-hour gap, with useful overlap mostly in late evenings.  ·  No direct user access — we couldn't user-test with actual Somali users during the build phase.  ·  Language gap — all stakeholder communication was in English, requiring extra care in specs and async documentation.

These weren't problems to solve once — they were the operating environment for 4 months. Every decision had to account for all of them simultaneously.

04 Key Decisions & Trade-offs

Three moments where the project could have gone wrong

1
The deleted server — absorb the loss or push the deadline?

Midway through the build, the client deleted our test server — they assumed it was idle and unused. It wasn't. We had CI/CD pipelines, deployment configs, and OPS setup running on it. All of it was gone overnight.


Three options were on the table:

Option A

Push the deadline by 2–3 weeks. Buy time to rebuild carefully.

✓ Chosen

Rebuild infra in parallel with feature dev. OT to close the gap. Keep the deadline.

Option C

Escalate to management and renegotiate contract terms.

Why we chose Option B: The infra rebuild, while painful, was a parallel workstream — it didn't block feature development. The team was senior enough to execute both tracks simultaneously. Pushing the deadline risked the client's marketing campaign and would have damaged trust early in the relationship. We held a team meeting, decided together, and committed to the OT.


What I learned: A PM's job in a crisis isn't to find the perfect solution — it's to give the team a clear decision fast, so they can stop worrying about the decision and start executing.

2
Scope creep in month 2 — add it, delay it, or phase it?

At the start of month 2, the client requested adding a Laundry module to the original 4-module scope. At the same time, they announced they wanted to begin marketing before full app launch — meaning some modules needed to be live and stable earlier than planned.


Adding Laundry to the current sprint would have stretched the team thin. Refusing it risked the client relationship. We spent one full day in an internal planning session before bringing a proposal to the client.

Phase 1 · Launch
Food, Delivery, Grocery, Pharmacy
4 core modules → marketing campaign starts
Phase 2 · Follow
Laundry Module
New scope → added post-launch without blocking Phase 1

The outcome: Client agreed. Marketing launched on schedule with the 4-module app. Laundry was added as a follow-on scope. Neither track blocked the other.


What I learned: Scope changes mid-project aren't the enemy — unmanaged scope changes are. A structured proposal that showed the client exactly what they gain (earlier marketing, cleaner launch) made it easy for them to say yes to our phased approach.

3
3 AM meetings — how do you manage a client who can't sync in your timezone?

The 4-hour timezone gap between Vietnam and Somalia sounds small. In practice, it meant our overlap window was late evening VN time — and some daily syncs landed at 3–4 AM for us. This wasn't sustainable for a 4-month run.


Instead of grinding through daily late-night calls, we shifted to an async-first process:

How we solved the timezone problem

Written agenda sent the night before every sync — so the Somalia side came prepared and we didn't waste time on context-setting at midnight.  ·  Decision log in Notion — every key decision was written down immediately after a call, eliminating "I thought we agreed on X" misunderstandings.  ·  Sync calls reserved for blockers only — routine updates moved to async. This alone cut weekly late-night calls from 5 to 2.

What I learned: Remote international PM requires thinking about communication as a system, not just a schedule. The goal isn't to be always available — it's to make sure nothing critical goes unresolved for more than 24 hours.

06 What I'd Do Differently

Three things I now do on every project

Establish infrastructure ownership on day one. The server deletion happened because there was no clear protocol for who could modify the environment. I now document environment ownership and change-approval rules in the kickoff doc — not as a formality, but as a genuine safeguard.

Treat scope change requests as a product opportunity, not a threat. The Laundry module addition could have derailed the project. Instead, it became an opportunity to propose a phased launch that actually helped the client's marketing timeline. When clients ask for more, the first question should be: "How does this create value, and what's the right sequencing?"

Design the communication system before the first meeting. Async-first isn't about being unavailable — it's about making information flow without requiring everyone to be online at the same time. A shared decision log and pre-written agendas cut confusion more than any number of additional calls.

Reflection

This project taught me that shipping software internationally isn't primarily a technical challenge — it's a coordination challenge. The code was solvable. The hard part was building enough trust with a client 4 time zones away that they felt confident we were on top of things, even when things went wrong. That trust is what made the +$1K bonus possible — not because we were perfect, but because we were transparent and kept delivering.
